Three order queues.
One list for the kitchen.

How two lightweight AI agents on top of an ordinary Google Sheet freed a café's kitchen from manually re-tallying orders — no separate POS system, no new interface for staff to learn.

The problem

Orders arrive grouped by customer. The kitchen needs the opposite.

Three sources feed orders in at once: a customer at the counter, a form on the website, a phone call. Each order looks like one person's list of dishes — "Ivan: salad, sandwich, croissant." The kitchen needs the reverse cut: how many salads, sandwiches, and croissants need cooking right now, and in what order.

That recalculation used to be done by a person — in their head or on paper, between other tasks. That produced extra dishes, delays, and no real prioritization: it wasn't clear what to cook first.

The fix: two agents, one spreadsheet

No new system to learn — just two processes running on top of the spreadsheet staff already use.

Agent 1 pulls orders from all three sources into one sheet, marks them new, and archives them by day and month.
Agent 2 pulls only the new orders every 30 minutes, re-tallies them by dish, and updates the kitchen's working list — accounting for what's already been cooked and what hasn't.

What happens every 30 minutes

The "Total status" column is the only field the kitchen touches by hand. Everything else syncs on its own.

  • done and new orders for that dish arrived → the quantity is recalculated from scratch, status resets to new.
  • a number is set (the kitchen noted how much is still left to cook) → the new batch is added on top of that number.
  • no new orders arrived → the row is left untouched, and if it was done, it sinks to the bottom of the list.
